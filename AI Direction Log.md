# AI Direction Log — Loom to Bloom

---

## Entry 1 — First Build

**What I asked AI to do:**
I shared my Design Intent document and the project brief and asked Claude to build the full website from scratch — the intro collage, the five panels, the hover interaction, the fonts, colors, and timing.

**What it made:**
A working single page website (V1) with a collage intro, five equal panels that expand on hover, and gold text on a dark maroon background. It used placeholder images from the internet and a font called Hahmlet that wasn't in my design brief.

**What I kept:**
The three stage layout, the panel expansion on hover, and the color system. The basic structure was right.

**What I changed:**
The intro was fading away too fast — only 1.8 seconds. I also uploaded my actual five panel images to replace the placeholders.

**Why:**
1.8 seconds wasn't enough time to actually read the title or take in the collage. It felt like a quick flash rather than a proper cinematic opening. And the placeholder images had nothing to do with my project so the whole mood was off.

---

## Entry 2 — Timing Fix and Real Images

**What I asked AI to do:**
Three things: make the intro stay on screen longer (3.5 seconds), make the "hover to explore" hint text disappear on its own after a few seconds, and swap in my actual panel images.

**What it made:**
V2 — the intro now stays for 3.5 seconds then fades out over 0.8 seconds. The hint text fades in and then disappears automatically at 9 seconds. My real images were embedded directly into the file.

**What I kept:**
All three changes. The timing felt much better — the intro finally had the cinematic feel I was going for.

**What I changed:**
Even with the fade, I still didn't like the hint text. It felt out of place.

**Why:**
The website is supposed to feel like an editorial fashion experience — quiet, immersive, atmospheric. A little instruction label at the bottom broke that feeling. I wanted the interaction to feel natural, not like something the user needed to be told to do.

---

## Entry 3 — Collage Images

**What I asked AI to do:**
I uploaded 20 reference images — editorial photography, textiles, architecture — and asked Claude to build a proper mosaic collage using all of them, with different sized cells so it didn't look like a rigid grid.

**What it made:**
V3 — a collage grid with 6 columns and 5 rows where some images are taller than others. All 20 images were used and the result felt layered and atmospheric, like a mood board rather than a photo grid.

**What I kept:**
Everything. This one came out exactly the way I wanted it.

**Why it worked:**
I gave very specific direction — 20 specific images, varied sizing, mosaic feel. When the direction is clear and detailed, the output matches the vision.

---

## Entry 4 — Typography

**What I asked AI to do:**
Two separate changes: first switch the body font to Helvetica Neue, then make the collection name labels on the five panels use Cormorant Garamond.

**What it made:**
Two versions — V5 with Helvetica Neue for the body text, and V6 with Cormorant Garamond applied specifically to the panel labels.

**What I kept:**
Both changes. Helvetica Neue felt clean and modern for the supporting text. Cormorant Garamond on the panel labels made the collection names feel editorial and fashion-forward rather than like a basic UI element.

**What I changed:**
After seeing V6, I noticed that the text inside the hover state wasn't working. The brand name, collection name, designer name, and description were all roughly the same size and weight — nothing stood out as more important than anything else. The body copy was also too small to read.

**Why:**
My Design Intent has a clear hierarchy for the hover text — the brand name should be the biggest and most dominant, then the collection name, then the designer, then the description. What I got was a flat block of text where everything felt equal.

---

## Entry 5 — Image Framing

**What I asked AI to do:**
I wanted the five panel images to look visually consistent when the user first sees Stage 2 — all the figures at a similar level, garments fully visible, nothing awkwardly cropped.

**What it made:**
Several attempts across V9 and V10 trying to reframe each image individually. Each version looked worse than the one before — some panels had strange gaps, the figures sat at different heights, and the whole screen felt uneven.

**What I kept:**
None of the framing changes.

**What I changed:**
I rejected everything and went back to the version before any of the changes were applied.

**Why:**
After seeing multiple attempts that all made it worse, I realised the original default framing was actually better. The images were consistent and readable before I started trying to fix them. Sometimes the right call is to stop changing something and go back to what was already working.

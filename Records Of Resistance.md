# Records of Resistance — Loom to Bloom

---

## Record 1 — Removing the Hint Text

**Versions:** V2 → V4

**What AI produced:**
A "Hover to explore each collection" label sitting at the bottom of the screen. The AI added it on its own, it assumed that the users would need it.

**Why I rejected it:**
It was visually disturbing. The whole point of the website was for it to feel like an editorial fashion experience. A little instruction label at the bottom made it feel like an app tutorial instead.

**What I did instead:**
First I asked for it to fade out on its own after a few seconds. But even that didn't feel right. So I asked for it to be removed entirely. I wanted the interaction to feel natural and something the users discover on their own.

**What I learned:**
AI tends to add helpful little features like this automatically because it thinks they improve the experience, but not always helpful things help the aesthetic and mood. Part of being an art director is knowing when to say no to something that technically makes sense but feels wrong.

## Record 2 — Rejecting the Image Framing Attempts

**Versions:** V9 & V10 → rejected → V11

**What AI produced:**
I gave two attempts to AI. I asked it to adjust how the panel images were cropped and positioned. Each time I gave it a different direction — first I described what I wanted and then the second time I gave it a reference image. Each time the result looked worse.

**Why I rejected it:**
Both attempts made the panels look more broken and not better. The approach the AI was using was causing layout issues.

**What I did instead:**
Instead of giving it more specifications, I stepped back and just described the problem. The images were still looking visually inconsistent when placed beside each other and I asked it to revert back to the way the panels were looking in Version 8.

**What I learned:**
Sometimes the more direction you give, the worse the result gets. I kept trying to fix the problem by giving more specific instructions but the images kept looking worse. What actually worked was stepping back, describing the problem and letting the AI find its own solution.

## Record 3 — Fixing the Hover Text Hierarchy

**Versions:** V6 → V7 → V8

**What AI produced:**
After the font changes were made, the hover state had all four pieces of text — brand name, collection name, designer name and description. The text was roughly placed at the same size and weight. Everything looked equally important, there was no hierarchy. The description text was so small it was almost unreadable.

**Why I rejected it:**
My Design Intent was very clear about how the hover text should work. The brand name should be the biggest and most dominant, then the collection name, then the designer and then the description. What I got was a flat block of text where nothing stood out. It didn't feel like an editorial reveal.

**What I did instead:**
I asked the AI to adjust the sizes and weights to reflect a proper visual hierarchy.

**What I learned:**
AI plays it safe with typography by default and keeps everything in a similar range. As art director, my job was to push the contrast further and make the necessary changes to the font sizes and weights.
